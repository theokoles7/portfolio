Personal Background

I was born and raised in Louisiana, where I developed an early and persistent interest in how things work — not just mechanically, but structurally. That curiosity never stayed confined to any one domain. I have always been drawn to problems that sit at the edges of disciplines, where the tools of one field illuminate something unexpected in another. Growing up, I was equally at home taking things apart to see how they fit together and losing hours to a book or a puzzle that refused to resolve cleanly. That disposition — equal parts analytical and restless — has followed me into everything I do.

Outside of research and work, I am deeply interested in the dynamics of nature, specifically botany, entymology, and ornithology. I think of curiosity as a practice rather than a trait, something that has to be exercised across domains to stay sharp in any of them.

Academic Journey

My academic path has been less a straight line than a narrowing spiral — each pass returning to the same questions with sharper tools. I came to computer science through a genuine fascination with formal systems, and to machine learning through the realization that it offered a unique window into some of the deepest questions about knowledge, generalization, and structure. What draws me to this field is not the engineering, though I find that rewarding too, but the underlying epistemology: what does it mean for a system to learn something, rather than merely approximate it?

Over time, my interests have converged on a particular conviction — that systematicity matters. Not as an aesthetic preference, but as a methodological commitment. Principled, composable approaches to learning tend to reveal structure that ad hoc methods obscure, and that structure is often where the most interesting science lives. My research proceeds from that conviction, asking not just whether a method works, but why it works, under what conditions, and what that tells us about learning more broadly.

I am currently a doctoral candidate at the University of Louisiana at Lafayette, where I work at the intersection of reinforcement learning, curriculum learning, training dynamics, and the question of how the structure of a learning process shapes what a model ultimately becomes.
